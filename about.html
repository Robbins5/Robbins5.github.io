Why it matters

Traditional audit assumes stable systems and predictable workflows. Transition breaks those assumptions—and that’s where value leaks.

Repeat findings signal misalignment
Credibility

When the same control gaps appear in successive engagements, the problem is not management indifference. It is an audit methodology that has not kept pace with how the business now operates.

Data constraints compound over time
Access

Routing audit data through intermediary teams that don’t own validation creates a structural barrier to the quality of work that recovery requires.

Late visibility is a governance risk
Relevance

When audit finds out about new pilots, system changes, and operational redesigns after they are already underway, the window to influence controls has already closed.

The perspective behind this platform

This platform was built by a practitioner, not a consultant. The frameworks here reflect real decisions being worked through inside a real audit function -- which is the only reason they are worth reading.

Author
Nicole Robbins
Perspective
Built from the inside out

My name is Nicole Robbins. I'm an internal audit and analytics leader at a large national electrical distributor, where I lead the audit function's data science capability across an operation spanning hundreds of locations nationwide and multiple subsidiaries. My work sits at the intersection of audit methodology, analytical infrastructure, and governance -- translating complex data into risk intelligence that operations and executive leadership can act on.

I came to internal audit through an unconventional path, and that path is the point. Over twelve years at the same organization, I've held roles across supply chain, HR analytics, strategic accounts, and audit -- building an operational understanding of distribution businesses from the inside out. That cross-functional foundation shapes how I read risk, evaluate controls, and identify where value leaks when enterprise systems change. Most audit professionals develop this perspective from the outside looking in. I built it from the ground up.

I've spent my career leading people, owning processes, and driving outcomes across functions. Early in my career I managed legal operations across multiple practice areas -- designing and launching an internship program, owning the customer experience strategy, and establishing the processes and documentation that made a complex operation run. That experience shaped how I think about accountability, team development, and operational discipline -- and it is the lens I bring to audit leadership today.

In my current role, I've designed and built complex analytics that have surfaced anomalies supporting investigations into potential fraud, identified segregation of duties exposures, uncovered operational inefficiencies, flagged training gaps, and quantified financial leakage. That work was built on deep familiarity with the underlying data and the business processes behind it. An enterprise system transition has reset the infrastructure, and rebuilding it intentionally is the challenge I'm actively working through now. The frameworks on this platform reflect that work in real time.

My conviction is that the audit functions best positioned to deliver value in the next decade are the ones rebuilding their infrastructure intentionally today -- and that the window to do that well is narrow. I'm genuinely excited by what becomes possible when audit functions stop operating on a cycle and start operating as a continuous intelligence capability. That is the future this platform is built around.

My background extends beyond audit in ways that matter. Early experience in legal management and forensic analysis, combined with years working across HR and operational functions, developed my instincts for evidentiary rigor, investigative methodology, and behavioral pattern recognition. I've learned to read not just what the data shows, but what human behavior behind the data suggests -- which is often where the most significant audit insights live. Those instincts inform how I approach audit evidence quality today, particularly in environments where data integrity is contested and findings need to be airtight.

I'm pursuing a Master of Science in Human-Centered AI at Lindenwood University -- an area with direct implications for how organizations govern automated decision-making, maintain audit trails in AI-enabled environments, and ensure that human accountability doesn't disappear as intelligent systems take on more operational responsibility.

I currently serve as Professional Development Chair for WINGS (Women Influencing Graybar's Success) and previously served as Vice President of the Women in Industry Steering Committee, a cross-industry leadership initiative connecting women across distributors and manufacturers nationwide. Outside of work I contribute to my community through hands-on volunteer work with Rebuilding Together, Habitat for Humanity, Sleep in Heavenly Peace, and the YMCA.

If you are working through similar challenges in your audit function, I'd welcome the conversation.
