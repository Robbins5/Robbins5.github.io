The burning platform

Post-ERP go-live, most audit functions face the same reality: the infrastructure they relied on no longer exists in the same form. The stronger play is to treat the disruption as a design opportunity.

Disruption is also a design opportunity.

Post-ERP go-live, most audit functions face the same reality: the data infrastructure they relied on no longer exists in the same form. Legacy analytics workflows break. Data structures change. Access models get locked down under new governance frameworks. For many teams, this triggers a scramble to recreate what was lost. The stronger play is to treat the disruption as a design opportunity -- to rebuild audit infrastructure intentionally rather than reactively.

Why ERP transitions are high-risk for audit -- and the organization

Research consistently shows that audit functions that engage proactively during implementation emerge with stronger organizational standing than those that treat the transition as a threat.

Research finding
Credibility is won or lost at blueprint stage
Research from the Journal of Accounting Literature found that audit functions that treat ERP implementation as a threat to their legitimacy struggle to recover credibility, while those that engage proactively -- acquiring system knowledge, partnering with implementation teams, and delivering data-driven insights -- emerge with stronger organizational standing.
Protiviti guidance
The blueprint window is irreplaceable
Protiviti's guidance on ERP implementation identifies that internal audit and compliance personnel should be actively involved in blueprint workshops during system design, because identifying the internal control framework at the blueprint stage is far more efficient than remediation after go-live. Most audit functions miss this window. The ones that don't earn a seat at the table they will need for the next decade.
Strategic positioning
Proactive engagement reshapes the relationship
Audit functions that arrive as partners during system design -- contributing knowledge of control requirements, data ownership, and access governance -- are positioned differently than those that arrive after go-live asking for access. The distinction compounds over time in how leadership perceives the function's value.

The data access imperative

The 2024 IIA Global Internal Audit Standards are unambiguous: internal auditors must have unrestricted access to the data, records, and information necessary to fulfill the internal audit mandate.

A professional obligation, not a departmental preference
This is a mandatory requirement tied to Principle 6 -- Authorized by the Board -- and must be explicitly included in the audit charter. When ERP governance frameworks restrict audit data access through intermediary teams or limit query capabilities, they inadvertently create a conflict with professional standards.
The distinction that matters
Governance lockdowns target write access
Governance lockdowns are typically designed to prevent unauthorized write access. Read-only access for audit is architecturally consistent with those governance principles and does not implicate the risks that drove the lockdown. Audit's data access request is not a governance exception.
Charter requirement
Authorization exists at the board level
The IIA 2024 Standards require that unrestricted data access be explicitly included in the audit charter as a mandatory element. This means the authorization exists at the board level -- not at the discretion of the IT governance team or the ERP system owner.
Practical implication
The access request carries professional weight
Framing the data access request in standards language -- referencing the IIA mandatory requirement and the audit committee's responsibility under the charter -- changes the nature of the conversation from a budget request to a compliance question.

The intermediary data problem

Routing audit data requests through a reporting or data delivery team creates a structural problem that does not exist in operational reporting contexts.

Exploratory work cannot run on a fixed-report delivery model.

Audit work is inherently exploratory and iterative -- the data question at the start of an engagement is rarely the same as the question at the end, because following the evidence is the point. A delivery model built for fixed, repeatable reports cannot support this workflow. More importantly, when the team delivering data does not own validation of what it provides, audit bears the evidence accountability but lacks the access needed to validate efficiently.

The result is a loop of requests, tickets, validation failures, redelivery, and delays -- at precisely the moment when audit needs to move. The ICAEW identifies data quality as the single greatest risk to analytics-driven audit credibility, noting that trust built through analytical findings can be rapidly lost when the underlying data is unreliable.

A realistic rebuild timeline: 1 to 3 years

Recovery and rebuilding takes one to three years when managed deliberately. Each phase has distinct objectives, an interim operating model, and expected outputs.

Phase 1 -- Months 1 to 6 post go-live
Stabilization and Assessment
Organizations going through ERP transitions need time to stabilize. Audit should use this window productively, not passively. Map the new system's data structures. Document which legacy analytics apply and which need to be rebuilt or retired. Submit the formal data access request with professional standards language. Catalog access gaps specifically so the request is precise, not general.
Expected outputs: Data gap assessment, formal access request submitted, preliminary engagement plan for the first post-stabilization audit
Phase 2 -- Months 7 to 18
Interim Access and Resumed Engagements
This is where engagements resume under constrained access conditions. The interim workflow should be negotiated explicitly: audit submits field-level data specifications, the delivery team produces extracts, and audit retains the right to validate against source transactions using whatever direct access exists. Audit documents this validation as part of the working paper file. Simultaneously, the business case for expanded direct access -- including native SAP reporting tools and read-only access to validated data models -- moves through approval. In companies that move deliberately, expect this to take longer than you want.
Expected outputs: Two to three post-ERP engagements completed, access expanded beyond initial limitations, continuous monitoring business case drafted
Phase 3 -- Months 18 to 36
Continuous Monitoring Infrastructure
By this stage, the goal is a functioning continuous monitoring capability for the highest-risk process areas, data access that no longer requires ticketing through an intermediary, and audit analytics workflows that can be operated across the team. Native SAP tools such as Business Integrity Screening -- which provides out-of-the-box automated controls for procure-to-pay, order-to-cash, and record-to-report processes with anomaly detection and machine learning -- represent the leading-edge option for organizations operating on S/4HANA. These tools draw from the system's own validated data model, eliminating the extraction and validation problem entirely while producing output compatible with Microsoft Power BI for reporting.
Expected outputs: Continuous monitoring active for one to two high-risk process areas, direct data access established, analytics capability distributed beyond a single team member

The access request that moves organizations

Frame the data access request as a professional obligation and an organizational risk issue, not a departmental preference.

The disclosure obligation creates urgency without escalation.
The 2024 IIA Standards require the chief audit executive to disclose to the audit committee any interference in determining the scope of internal auditing or performing audit work. Unresolved data access limitations that materially constrain audit's ability to fulfill its mandate qualify as scope limitations under this standard. That is not a threat. It is a procedural requirement that leadership should understand before the disclosure becomes necessary.
