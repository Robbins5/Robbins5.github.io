AI Research Tools Are Influencing Decision-Making Without Formal Oversight

January 25, 2026

AI research tools that summarize documents, synthesize information, and answer complex questions are increasingly used by leaders to navigate large volumes of operational and financial data. Vendor demonstrations and practitioner use cases show these tools acting as intermediaries between raw information and executive judgment, often without being formally designated as decision-support systems.

In industries such as electrical distribution, leadership decisions rely heavily on accurate forecasting, supplier performance data, customer demand trends, and working capital metrics. Publications from Supply Chain Brain and the National Association of Electrical Distributors consistently emphasize the importance of timely and reliable information in managing supply chain volatility. When AI tools summarize or interpret this information, they influence what leaders see and prioritize.

This trend intersects with Institute of Internal Auditors Standard 2120, which focuses on risk identification and assessment, and COSO principles related to the use of relevant and reliable information. If AI-generated insights are incomplete, biased, or misunderstood, decision-making risk increases even if the underlying data systems remain unchanged.

What stands out is that many organizations have not yet established guidance for AI-assisted research and analysis. These tools are often treated as productivity aids rather than as components of the information flow that shapes executive awareness and risk perception.

AI advisory boards can help define acceptable use cases, validation expectations, and disclosure requirements. Internal audit teams must also adjust by expanding beyond traditional report reviews and developing the capability to assess how AI-generated insights influence risk awareness and operational decisions, even when those insights are informally produced.
